Australia - The State of Renewables

Australia stands at a critical juncture in its energy history. By the end of 2024, coal's share in electricity generation dropped below 50% for the first time, a watershed moment for a nation that has built its prosperity on fossil fuel exports. This visualization maps the geographic, technological, and temporal dimensions of Australia's renewable energy transition, revealing both the remarkable progress achieved and the substantial challenges that lie ahead.

Two key measurements help us understand this transformation. A megawatt (MW) measures instantaneous power capacity, how much electricity a facility can generate at peak performance. A gigawatt-hour (GWh) measures actual energy production over time, capturing what was delivered to homes and businesses. The distinction matters: a solar farm might have high MW capacity but lower annual GWh output due to nighttime and weather variability.

Overview: Australia's Renewable Landscape

Australia's renewable installations of 10 MW and larger cluster dramatically along the eastern seaboard, reflecting both population density and grid infrastructure that evolved over a century of centralized coal-fired generation. The concentration is no coincidence—connecting renewable projects to the existing grid remains far more economically viable than building entirely new transmission corridors to remote locations, however abundant their wind or solar resources might be.

AEMO's 2024 Integrated System Plan forecasts that an estimated 4,581 km of new transmission lines are needed to meet 2030 targets, with some scenarios requiring double that by 2050. This represents Australia's most acute infrastructure bottleneck. The federal government's $20 billion Rewiring the Nation program aims to address this through priority projects like VNI West (connecting Victoria and New South Wales) and Marinus Link (Tasmania to Victoria), but transmission costs have increased by approximately 25% to 55% in real terms compared to 2024 estimates after accounting for inflation, driven by competition for labour and materials across the entire economy.

The map also reveals Australia's renewable energy zones (REZs)—designated areas with strong resources and streamlined planning approvals designed to accelerate development. Yet progress remains uneven: in New South Wales, large renewable project approvals take four to seven years and cost over $4 million, while Queensland's system takes just one to two years and costs $30,000. This regulatory fragmentation across states creates perverse outcomes where projects cluster in jurisdictions with faster approvals rather than optimal resource locations.

State-by-State Comparison

Total electricity generation reveals Australia's fundamental regional inequality. New South Wales and Queensland generate the most energy simply because they have the largest populations and industrial bases, but their grids remain heavily dependent on aging coal plants. Victoria's substantial brown coal fleet at Latrobe Valley has been in managed decline, with the catastrophic Hazelwood closure in 2017 removing 1,600 MW overnight and sending shockwaves through the market.

Tasmania tells a different story entirely—virtually 100% renewable through hydroelectricity from schemes built decades ago. South Australia has achieved something even more remarkable: transitioning from coal dependence to majority renewable generation within 15 years, now regularly operating at 70-80% instantaneous renewable penetration. This wasn't without pain—the state experienced a statewide blackout in 2016 during the transition, forcing hard lessons about system stability that the rest of Australia is now applying.

The interstate transmission constraints visible in this data create market distortions and reliability challenges. South Australia often generates more renewable energy than it can use domestically but faces limits on how much can flow to other states through the interconnectors. Some solar farms experienced curtailment rates above 25% in 2024, with generators literally switching off during midday peaks because the grid cannot absorb the power. Tasmania's massive hydro resources could firm the entire eastern seaboard's variable renewables, but the proposed Marinus Link connecting it to Victoria faces years of planning battles and cost escalations.

Technology Breakdown

When we break down renewable generation by technology, wind and solar's complementary patterns become clear. South Australia's wind resources are exceptional—consistent, strong, and located relatively close to grid infrastructure. Queensland and New South Wales are rapidly scaling solar, benefiting from cheaper technology costs and faster construction times. Australia produced 92,700 GWh of renewable energy in 2024, an increase of more than 30% since 2021, with rooftop solar alone adding 3.2 GW of new capacity.

But this technology mix creates new system management challenges. Solar generation peaks in spring and summer afternoons then disappears entirely overnight. Wind varies by weather patterns and seasons. Hydroelectricity depends on rainfall and dam management—Tasmania's output can swing dramatically between wet and dry years. As at the end of 2024, there was over 8.7 GW/23.3 GWh of large-scale battery capacity under construction, essential infrastructure to time-shift renewable generation and provide grid stability services that coal plants once delivered through their rotating mass.

Major Players

The 20 largest renewable power stations by installed MW capacity reveal which technologies are achieving scale. Large wind farms dominate the top ranks—projects like Stockyard Hill (530 MW) and Coopers Gap (453 MW) represent investments of over $1 billion each and require years of development, community consultation, and grid connection studies before construction even begins.

These mega-projects face intense scrutiny from local communities concerned about visual impact, noise, and property values. Unlike coal plants that employed hundreds of permanent workers, large renewable installations typically need only a handful of ongoing operations staff after construction, fundamentally changing regional employment patterns. Financial investment in large-scale renewable generation projects skyrocketed by 500% in 2024, jumping from $1.5 billion in 2023 to $9 billion, but this capital flows differently through regional economies than the multi-decade industrial operations that coal mining and power generation represented.

Grid integration at this scale requires sophisticated forecasting and coordination. The Australian Energy Market Operator now manages a system with thousands of distributed generators rather than a few dozen predictable coal plants. Weather forecasting has become critical infrastructure—predicting tomorrow's wind speeds and cloud cover now determines grid stability and wholesale electricity prices more than fuel availability ever did.

Two Decades of Transformation

The area chart spanning 2000 to 2024 tells Australia's energy transformation story in stark visual terms. Coal's share fell from 64% a decade ago to 47% in 2022-23 and 46% in calendar year 2023, with the decline accelerating as plants reach end-of-life and become economically unviable against zero-marginal-cost renewables. The coal generation dominating the chart's early years represents not just electricity but Australia's industrial identity—the Hunter Valley and Latrobe Valley built entire communities around coal, with generational employment and social infrastructure all tied to an industry now in terminal decline. Victoria's brown coal is among the most emissions-intensive in the world, making its reduction particularly significant for climate commitments.

Wind's steady climb from negligible in 2000 to substantial contributor by 2024 reflects the bipartisan Renewable Energy Target that provided investment certainty through the 2010s, demonstrating how policy stability drives private capital into infrastructure with 20-30 year lifespans. Solar's explosive surge after 2015 tells multiple overlapping stories: utility-scale farms became cost-competitive at USD 36 per MWh while Australia achieved the world's highest per-capita rooftop adoption—over 35% of Western Australian homes now have arrays. This distributed generation fundamentally changed grid management, as power now flows bidirectionally with residential areas exporting significant electricity midday.

Battery storage's emergence represents technological maturation, with $3.8 billion invested in 2024 alone following the success of Tesla's Hornsdale Power Reserve in South Australia. Hydro's relatively flat contribution masks critical roles—Tasmania's schemes and Snowy Hydro provide flexible generation that ramps up when wind and solar dip, though Snowy 2.0's troubled expansion now exceeds $20 billion in costs, more than ten times initial estimates.

Where To From Here?

The trajectory is clear but not inevitable. Achieving Australia's commitment of 82% renewable electricity by 2030 requires sustaining the recent investment pace while solving transmission constraints, managing grid stability with high renewable penetration, and ensuring just transitions for coal-dependent communities. The Australian government announced an additional funding of 1.2 billion euros for its "green bank," aimed at accelerating the transition, recognizing that despite remarkable progress, the hardest challenges lie ahead. The coal generation that remains is increasingly critical for system stability during periods of low renewable output, creating a complex phaseout dance where too-early closures risk blackouts but delayed closures lock in emissions and slow the transition.

Australia's energy transformation has progressed from impossible to inevitable in just two decades, but the final stretch—replacing the last coal plants while maintaining reliability and affordability—will test both our technical capabilities and social cohesion.
