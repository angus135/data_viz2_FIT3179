Main Title - Data Viz 2

This report guides the reader through Australia current renewable energy landscape using maps and charts that show where projects sit, how much capacity each state has, and how generation has changed over time. It is written for an average Australian with no prior technical background and uses simple explanations of terms where needed. A megawatt MW measures how much power a site can produce at a single moment while a gigawatt hour GWh measures how much energy is produced over time for example one GWh equals one thousand megawatt hours. The narrative begins with the map of major installations, then compares states and technologies, highlights large projects, and ends with an analysis of long term generation trends.

This map plots major renewable installations of 10 MW and larger across Australia and shows where different technologies are concentrated. Points are sized by installed capacity in megawatts MW and coloured by the primary fuel such as solar, wind and hydro so the map makes it easy to see both density and technology type. A megawatt MW is a common measure of generation capacity and indicates how much power a site can produce at a moment in time. The map shows most large projects clustered along the eastern and southeastern coast and on some regional corridors while more isolated projects appear in other states.

The area chart shows national energy generation by source from 2000 through 2024 and stacks each fuel so the reader can see both totals and how the mix has changed. The large brown area indicates historical dominance by coal while the rising bands of solar and wind toward the right show the growing contribution of renewable generation. Battery and biofuel contributions are smaller but visible in recent years reflecting new technologies and deployments. The vertical axis is energy in GWh so the reader can compare yearly energy production across sources.

Over the last two decades the chart shows a clear decline in coal generation alongside steady increases in wind and a rapid rise in solar generation especially after about 2015. Wind farms add consistent output over many years while solar has grown quickly because of large utility farms and rooftop installations. Battery storage appears as an emerging category and helps explain how variable renewables can be balanced during peak times. Hydro remains a steady, seasonally variable contributor and helps to firm supply in some regions.

Taken together the trends suggest the electricity system is moving from a few large thermal plants toward a more diverse mix that includes many renewable sites and increasing storage capacity. Continued growth of renewables will require more grid upgrades, more storage to manage variability and new approaches to system planning to keep supply reliable as old plants retire.
